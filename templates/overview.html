So you want to choose a programming language...

So many choices! How to pick? As a third year software developer, I have learned a few languages and have made some mistakes, and worked in the industry. This guide is designed for people who are coming to programming from a different job or career, and do not necessarily have the support, guidance, and structure of a university program. This guide does not tell you which languages will earn you the most money, because they will all earn you enough in the beginning, and you can make choices about earning power after you've been in the industry for a couple years. There are many languages, but to pick a first language, there are really only, in my opinion, three, maybe four good options. This is a good thing! Your job at the beginning is simple.

    Here are some things this site WON'T tell you:
  • Salaries associated with a language. Junior developers make a similar salary regardless of language. After you've spent a few years in the industry, you'll be able to target certain positions, salary ranges, and languages.
  • Percentages of software developers using a given language. This information can be useful, but it is also misleading to junior developers, since certain languages are used primarily by senior devs. (Skew effect)
  • 25 fascinating programming languages, none of which are a common entry point into the industry.

Also, I should note that any language you choose will lead you in a good direction. All languages share certain features, such as functions and fdjfkdj and fjdkjf, and once you are proficient in one, it is not difficult to learn another.

Finally, learning a computer language throughly takes many years. You don't need to learn everything at once. If you enjoy programming, and enjoy the industry, you will have a long time to get better and better.
